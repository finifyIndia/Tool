Why Emergency Fund Matters

An emergency fund protects you during job loss, medical issues, family emergencies or sudden repairs. Without it, even a small emergency can push you into high-interest credit card debt or personal loans.

Credit Score Impact

If expenses and EMIs are not controlled, payments can get delayed. Late EMI or credit card payments can reduce your credit score and make future loans costlier or harder to get.

Expense Ratio Discipline

A healthy plan keeps essential expenses, EMI and savings balanced. When EMI is too high, savings reduce, emergency fund becomes weak and future goals get delayed.

Common Questions

How many months of emergency fund should I keep?

Usually 6 months of essential expenses is a strong target. If your income is unstable or you have high EMI, 9 to 12 months is safer.

Is EMI bad for my financial health?

EMI is not always bad. It becomes risky when total EMI takes too much of monthly income and reduces savings or emergency fund contribution.

What is a good savings rate?

Try to save and invest at least 20% of income. If possible, 30% or more gives stronger long-term financial freedom.

Should I buy a car or bike if my emergency fund is low?

It is better to first build at least 3 months of emergency fund before taking a new vehicle loan. A weak emergency fund plus new EMI can create pressure.

Why does credit score matter?

A good credit score can help you get loans faster and at better interest rates. Missed payments and high credit usage can damage your score.

Should I save for goals or emergency fund first?

First build a basic emergency fund, then balance goal savings and investments. For big goals like home or car, avoid using your full emergency fund.
